Te Māramataka

The Māori Lunar Calendar

The Māramataka varies between iwi and regions. This reference draws from widely shared traditions. Consult your own iwi or hapū traditions for local practice.

The Māramataka is the Māori lunar calendar. Each month (marama) follows the moon through 29 named nights. The quality of each night guides what to plant and when to rest. The cycle repeats every month, but the stars overhead and the season on the land shift as the year turns.

The Lunar Cycle

Within each month, the moon passes through 29 named nights. Some are favourable for planting and fishing. Others demand rest. Four patterns shape the cycle:

The Tamatea Nights

Nights 6, 7, 8

Three nights of unsettled energy around the first quarter moon. Named for the atua Tamatea. Avoid the sea and important tasks.

Rākaunui

Night 15

The full moon. The most favourable night of the entire cycle. Everything planted or begun on Rākaunui thrives.

The Korekore Void

Nights 19, 20, 21

The void. The moon wanes and energy drains. Rest, reflect, and wait for the turn toward Tangaroa.

The Tangaroa Nights

Nights 22, 23, 24

The god of the sea is ascendant. Excellent for all fishing and ocean activities. The waning moon draws fish to the surface.
